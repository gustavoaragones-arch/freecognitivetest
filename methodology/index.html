How cognitive screening works (on this site)

Cognitive screening in clinical settings often samples several mental skills in a brief session. Our educational tools follow similar themes so visitors can learn what clinicians may look for—without claiming clinical accuracy or normative scoring.

  • Recall — remembering words or patterns after a short delay (for example word recall and memory hub exercises).
  • Attention — sustaining focus on a target despite distraction (digit span-style tasks and attention drills in brain exercises).
  • Executive function — planning, sequencing, and switching between rules (trail-making-style demos and everyday planning guides).
  • Visuospatial skills — arranging or copying spatial information (clock drawing layout, visual grid tasks).
  • Processing speed — how quickly and accurately you complete timed steps (matching, scanning, and reaction-style demos).

Each exercise is intentionally short (often about two minutes) so users can repeat it for learning—not to produce a clinical score comparable to a neuropsychological battery.

Educational positioning

These tools are educational cognitive screening exercises and are not a medical diagnosis.

FreeCognitiveTest.org is designed for self-awareness, caregiver education, and structured practice. A low or high result on any page here does not prove health or disease. If memory, thinking, or behavior changes worry you or someone you support, speak with a qualified healthcare professional who can review history, medications, mood, sleep, and formal testing if needed.

Test origin transparency

Several tasks on this site are inspired by screening approaches that appear frequently in primary care and research literature. We describe them in plain language so users understand the idea behind each exercise.

  • Mini-Cog (concept) — combines brief word recall with a clock-drawing step. Our Mini-Cog walkthrough is an educational demo, not an official Mini-Cog® administration or scored clinical protocol.
  • Clock drawing (concept) — asks users to place numbers and hands in a circle, which can surface visuospatial and planning difficulties in professional settings. Our clock drawing canvas is for practice and learning, not a licensed clinical form.
  • Trail Making (concept) — involves connecting ordered targets under time pressure, reflecting processing speed and mental flexibility. Our trail-making-style demo is simplified for the browser and is not equivalent to published Trail Making Test norms.
  • Other demos — digit span, word lists, visual grids, and self-check questionnaires illustrate domains clinicians sometimes discuss; they are not substitutes for validated instruments.

We do not claim partnerships with test publishers, hospitals, or guideline bodies. Naming is used only to explain widely discussed screening approaches, not to imply endorsement or certification.

Limitations

Please interpret every result with these constraints in mind:

  • Device and screen — small screens, touch targets, and browser differences can affect timing and drawing accuracy.
  • Fatigue and timing — sleep loss, illness, stress, or testing late in the day can lower performance without indicating a chronic condition.
  • Language and literacy — word-based tasks may be harder for multilingual users or those with low vision; we offer some accessible layouts but not full clinical accommodations.
  • Distractions — home environments with interruptions differ from quiet clinic rooms.
  • No population norms — we do not publish age- or education-adjusted cutoffs tied to peer-reviewed validation on this platform.
  • Educational-only purpose — content supports learning; it must not be used to deny care, override clinician judgment, or self-treat serious symptoms.

Common questions

Is this a diagnosis?

No. Results are for education and practice only.

Should I trust a single low score?

One difficult day is common. Persistent change, safety concerns, or caregiver worry warrant professional advice—not conclusions from a website demo.
